Perspective 3: Daria

Daria has lived outside of the ‘new normal’ since childhood. Were her parents still alive, they would fall well within what is now referred to pejoratively as ‘neo-Ludditism’ - refusing to engage with the social media networks of the time and preferring to spend their free time enjoying analogue pursuits, they passed their scepticism of technology - its rapid expansion and ever-increasing power - on to her.

Excluding oneself from the convenience of an AI-assisted lifestyle doesn’t come easily. Refusing to engage with shopping assistant services means that food shopping has become an hours-long ordeal that Daria grudgingly undertakes biweekly with her partner, Rina.

“They scour the surface of each package to find the last remaining pieces of human-readable text that describe what is inside.”

Rosy memories of shopping trolley rides through aisles of ordered but vibrant products with her parents have been mostly replaced by current-day attempts to memorise the labyrinthine and ever-changing layout of their local CleanEat shop. Rina has recently begun drawing maps leading them to the most vital products, which tides them over for a few weeks, until a new product line is brought in which causes a full algorithmic reshuffle of the entire stock. Then they are back to square one, scouring the surface of each almost-identical rectangular package to find the last remaining pieces of human-readable text that describe what is inside.

Daria has taken her parents’ scepticism one step further and become an active part of the modern counterculture. By day, she works as a graphic designer in one of the few remaining human-led studios in the country. By night, she collaborates with other creatives on subversive designs that target AI’s stranglehold over modern life.

Before leaving the supermarket each week, she stops to surreptitiously plaster some of her newly-designed stickers across the shelves. These stickers are a two-pronged attack: a message to other humans who feel left behind by the continued march towards computer supremacy in daily life, as well as an ‘adversarial patch’ that disrupts those very systems when they perceive them.

It’s primitive, sure, and Daria has reservations about how these patches might affect the livelihoods of the poor gig economy workers who are forced to wear the headsets all day. But all rebellions start somewhere, right?
