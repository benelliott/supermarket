Perspective 2: Arthur

19:01

Arthur’s joints are creaking and he’s barely started his shift. On rainy days like these, the solar panels on the e-bikes can’t pull enough energy and he’s stuck pedalling manually like the old days.

A severe ‘ping’ erupts from his JenSys glasses: an order has come in, with delivery due at 20:00. The shop is 2km away and the recipient is another 7km from there, so he’s going to struggle to meet the order deadline - but at this time of the month, he has to take what he can get if he wants to keep up with the rent.

“Accept”, he states. The headset doesn’t respond.

“Accept!” Arthur has to act quickly or the order will be passed onto other Shopping Buddies in the vicinity. There is no sense of community among them, they aren’t a pack; just thousands of lone wolves each trying to keep themselves afloat.

He just has time to breathe a sigh of relief as the job state changes to ‘In progress’ and the augmented interface enters navigation mode. A gigantic arrow appears in mid-air above the street he’s currently looking down and an estimated arrival time is displayed in the corner, precise to the second. It continues ticking up as he turns his e-bike around to face the right direction and hops on.

“Arthur reaches the shopfront. From the outside, it’s barely recognisable as a supermarket.”

After seven years in the job, Arthur knows the route to CleanEat like the back of his hand. At this point, the gigantic augmented arrows and screeching navigation voiceovers are a safety hazard more than an aid - but the setting to switch them off was disabled in a software update some months ago after telemetry found that they reduced delivery times by 8% on average (to say nothing of accident rates).

Eventually, Arthur reaches the shopfront. From the outside, it’s barely recognisable as a supermarket. Any sense of branding has been eschewed in favour of machine comprehension. There is still a large signboard above the entrance, but ‘CleanEat’ is written in a neutral sans-serif and only occupies about a quarter of the space. The rest is taken up by overlapping barcodes and data encodings, each readable only to a specific AI. There was a standard once - encodings that could be processed by pretty much any computer vision algorithm on Earth - but that was before ideological feudalism erupted between AI manufacturers and mutual incompatibility was seen as the only viable outcome. In stark contrast to conservative rivals like TopThought and Comprehend, JenSys frames itself as a friendly, responsible AI corporation for the eco-conscious, liberal consumer. Arthur has worked for all three in his time; to him they’re all interchangeable.

Arthur steps through the door and his headset’s route navigation interface morphs into its indoor incarnation, directing him to successive items from a shopping list. His estimated arrival time continues to tick.

Unlike finding the shop itself, Arthur wouldn’t have a clue how to find the items without his headset. This is for two reasons. First, like the shopfront, every item’s packaging has had all sense of identity and human recognisability stripped from it in order to leave sufficient space for rivalling data encodings (fresh produce such as fruit were initially exempted from this practice until anecdotal reports of kiwis being provided instead of new potatoes caused a rethink). Second, because rather than cohesive aisles for ‘Dairy’, ‘Toiletries’ and so on, every piece of stock has been placed according to what was deemed optimal by an algorithm. Known as chaotic storage, this leaves aubergines next to hair dryers next to tins of rice pudding.

The vast majority of customers are AI-guided Shopping Buddies like Arthur, although he does spot a couple trying to buy their shopping their old way. They are scouring the aisles, consulting what looks like a hand-drawn map and squinting at the 10-point textual descriptors nestled between the abstract shapes on each package. These minuscule lines of human-readable text are all that government regulation mandates after successive rounds of corporate bribery.

Arthur has to admit that the headset does a great job of making this part easy. Through it, virtual wayfinding markings appear on the floor and lead him to specific aisles, shelves and then products. As soon as he picks it up and places it in his crate, it’s checked off the list and the next item is highlighted. The products have, of course, been listed in the most efficient order according to the organisation of the shop.

“His headset cuts to black. He’s temporarily in absolute darkness, in the middle of this supermarket, in the middle of the day.”

After picking up the last item in the list, a glimmer of colour catches Arthur’s eye and he glances at the aisle opposite. Stickers have been pasted on some of the products on this shelf - vibrant patterns of organic-looking shapes.

As he squints for a closer look, his headset cuts to black. He’s temporarily in absolute darkness, in the middle of this supermarket, in the middle of the day. He pulls the headset from his head and checks the status lights on the side. Rebooting.

A pang of panic runs through Arthur’s spine. Each minute behind cuts his earnings by a further 15%. He’s completely powerless without the headset - he has no idea what the customer’s address is, and he wouldn’t know how to get there if he did. All he can do is wait for the reboot sequence to complete.

As he waits, he looks at the sticker through his own eyes. As well as those strange patterns, the sticker has a message:

REHUMANISE OUR SOCIETY

He ponders this for a moment. This job consumes so much of his life that he rarely considers whether there could even be another way. Everyone else he sees seems to be silently complicit in the system as it is.

He heads outside and affixes the shopping crate to his bike. After several minutes, the headset pings a sign of life. He wastes no time in putting it back on.

His ETA is now 20.07.

It’s completely out of his control, but the customer won’t know that, and JenSys doesn’t care. All he can do is pedal as fast as he can.
